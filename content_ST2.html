Climate and the Ocean

Explore: Ocean Warming | Ocean Acidification

While we often focus on how the climate affects the air we breathe, the ocean is actually our greatest ally—and it’s taking a heavy hit. It absorbs about 90% of the excess heat and 30% of the carbon dioxide (CO2) produced by human activity.



1. Ocean Warming: The Underwater Heatwave



As the ocean traps heat, the consequences ripple through every marine ecosystem.



  • Thermal Expansion: As water gets warmer, it expands, contributing significantly to rising sea levels.
  • Coral Bleaching: Corals are highly sensitive. A temperature rise of just 1-2°C can cause them to expel the algae living in their tissues, turning them white and leaving them at risk of starvation.
  • Migration Shifts: Fish populations are moving toward the poles in search of cooler waters, disrupting local fishing economies.


2. Ocean Acidification: The "Osteoporosis" of the Sea



When the ocean absorbs CO2, a chemical reaction occurs that reduces the pH of the water, making it more acidic.



  • The Chemical Reaction: CO2 reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid (H2CO3), depleting carbonate ions.
  • Weakened Shells: Shellfish and tiny plankton struggle to build shells. In high-acidity environments, shells can literally begin to dissolve.
